What are we actually trying to improve?

functions, not format
The problem with TEEL and why swapping it for a new template won't work

TEEL works as a scaffold for beginners. The problem is that it teaches order rather than function — so students produce four-sentence templates even when their ideas need space to breathe.

What TEEL teaches

Sentence 1 = topic. Sentence 2 = evidence. Sentence 3 = explain. Sentence 4 = link. Every paragraph looks the same.

What this replaces it with

A paragraph needs a claim, proof, meaning, and a return — in whatever order serves the idea.

What replaces it? functions + the improvement routine

Two ideas to hold onto instead of a recipe:

  • Paragraph functions: a paragraph should do identifiable work — claim → proof → meaning → return.
  • Improvement routine: students get better through attempts + upgrades (MVP cycle), not waiting for a perfect draft.

Core principle: understand what your writing needs to include and how to improve it — not what order your sentences must follow.

How do I avoid 'structure creep'? the template test
  • If it can be taught as "Sentence 1/2/3…", it will become a new TEEL.
  • If success criteria are vague ("uses evidence"), students will default to summary.
  • If feedback is long, students won't resubmit — the cycle breaks.

Practical guardrail: focus on one upgrade per draft. If it can't be named in one sentence, it's too many moving parts at once.

Scaffold phases

fade-out, not removal

Each phase uses the same timed paragraph task — the support structures are reduced progressively as the functions become second nature.

Goal: Momentum and correct function. Not perfection. Rails are fully visible.

Phase 1 Timed paragraph · 20 minutes
Write one paragraph that includes: • A clear claim about the prompt • Two lines of proof (two different ways of proving the same idea) • A payoff line ("This matters because…") • A return to the prompt • Optional: a limitation (Yet / However) to add complexity No-stall rule: no quote? Use a remembered moment, pattern, or paraphrase. Submit by the end of the lesson.

Goal: Same functions expected — but recalled from memory. Rails reduced.

Phase 2 Timed paragraph · 20 minutes
Claim. Two lines of proof. Payoff (meaning, not summary). Return to prompt. Write without the checklist. No-stall rule still applies.

Goal: No checklist. Write from internalised functions. Support shifts to reflection questions you can use yourself or with a reader.

Phase 3 Timed paragraph · 20 minutes
Answer the prompt. Use evidence purposefully. Make meaning. (No checklist. No harness.)

Questions to ask yourself (or a peer)

What does your payoff line say about human nature or society — not just about this novel?
What's the cost of the thing you've described? Who pays it?
Does your second paragraph push the first forward, or does it restart?
If your evidence disappeared, would the claim still mean the same thing?
Where's the tension in your argument — what doesn't quite fit?
What word in your claim are you most committed to? Can you defend it?

Payoff lines

evidence → meaning
What payoff is in one teachable line

Payoff is the sentence where the writer states what the evidence suggests about values, responsibility, consequence, or mechanism — not what happened.

Summary (what happened)

"This shows gender roles are restrictive."

Payoff (what it suggests)

"This matters because Márquez frames gender as training — locking masculinity to honour and femininity to marriageability."

Payoff stems low threshold → high ceiling
  • Low threshold: This matters because… / What this suggests is… / So Márquez implies…
  • Mechanism: This works because… / This reveals how… / In effect, this turns ___ into ___.
  • Values: This exposes a society that values ___ over ___. / The cost of this is…
  • Implication: The consequence is that… / It becomes less about ___ and more about ___.
  • Complexity: However, this also suggests… / Yet this only holds because…

Voice = control. Clear stance, precise nouns, and thinking verbs that name what the author is doing — not just describing what happens.

In Márquez's novel, guilt does not simply follow memory; it actively organises it, shaping how the town reconstructs Santiago's death. Fragmented testimony suggests remembrance is inseparable from moral implication, because every version of events carries the pressure of who might have acted. One-line upgrade bank

MVP marking · fast and consistent

Rule: focus on one upgrade per draft. Identify the highest-leverage missing function and fix that first — one clear move at a time.

Payoff upgrades highest leverage — most students need these first
Move 1

Add one payoff line beginning "This matters because…" that states what your evidence suggests about responsibility, values, or consequence.

Move 2

Replace one summary sentence with a mechanism sentence: "This reveals how…" + state the process, not the conclusion.

Move 3

Swap "shows/explores" for a thinking verb that names what Márquez is doing: exposes, frames, normalises, undercuts, implicates.

Claim upgrades if you can write payoff but your opening is still vague
Move 4

Add a mechanism word to your claim: not "guilt shapes memory" but "guilt distorts memory by turning recollection into a form of self-protection."

Move 5

Replace one vague noun (society, theme, idea) with a precision noun: honour code, patriarchal logic, communal guilt, reputational economy.

Complexity upgrades when you're already writing well and want to push further
Move 6

Add a "Yet…" or "However…" sentence that names a limit or cost of the pattern you've identified — what your argument doesn't fully account for.

Move 7

End with a sentence that advances the idea, not one that restates it: not "Overall, Márquez explores guilt" but "Márquez therefore presents guilt as the mechanism by which a community can witness failure without ever confessing it."

Sustained thinking across paragraphs

advance, don't restart

Simple rule: each paragraph must advance the same central idea by extending, complicating, or widening — not restarting the essay.

→
Extend
Add a mechanism, cause, or deeper explanation. Push the idea further.
⟳
Complicate
Add tension, a limit, or a cost. What doesn't fully fit?
↑
Widen
Shift the lens. Individual → community. Personal → structural.
Worked example: guilt + memory (3 paragraphs) see how the same idea builds across an essay
Payoff line from each paragraph → what the next paragraph must do
Para 1
Claim
Payoff: "Memory becomes a moral defence mechanism — the past is retold to make failure bearable."
Establishes the mechanism. Next paragraph must do more than repeat this.
Para 2
Extend
Move: Add the mechanism behind the mechanism — why retelling works as protection.
Payoff: "The fragmented narrative structure itself mirrors this process — Márquez constructs a text that performs the same distortions his characters practice, implicating the reader in the town's collective evasion."
This extends by explaining how the novel's form participates in the same logic as the characters.
Para 3
Complicate
Move: Name the cost — what guilt can't actually do, even through memory.
Payoff: "Yet the novel's very existence as a re-investigation suggests that managed guilt is unstable — the community's stories require constant retelling precisely because they cannot contain the moral weight they are designed to suppress."
This complicates by showing the failure of the defence mechanism identified in Para 1.
Common mistake: the three-separate-ideas essay why it plateaus at B

Three-separate-ideas essay

Para 1: guilt
Para 2: honour
Para 3: fate

Each paragraph is complete in itself. There is no accumulated argument. The essay is a list.

One-idea essay (extended)

Para 1: guilt distorts memory
Para 2: the novel's form performs that distortion
Para 3: but distortion is unstable — guilt persists

Each paragraph adds to the same idea. By the end, the argument is more complex than it started.

FAQs

common questions
Writing FAQ for when you're moving beyond rigid templates
Do I have to follow a specific order?

No. You can open with evidence and build to your claim. You can complicate before you explain. The question is: does your paragraph contain a claim, proof, meaning, and a return? Not: are they in a specific order?

Can I open with evidence instead of a claim?

Yes — and it can be very effective. Opening with a pattern or moment from the text, then building to your interpretation, can feel more analytical than a flat topic sentence. Just make sure the claim arrives before the payoff, not after.

How long should my paragraph be?

Long enough to include all four functions with meaning — usually 4–7 sentences. A 3-sentence paragraph can work if the payoff is doing real work. A 10-sentence paragraph usually has repetition or summary that can be cut.

What if I don't remember an exact quote?

Use a paraphrase or a pattern instead. "The narrator's repeated references to what the town didn't do" is still evidence. The no-stall rule applies: write the proof you remember, not the proof you can't find. A paraphrased example with good analysis beats a memorised quote with no payoff.

What's the difference between proof and payoff?

Proof is what happens in the text. Payoff is what that suggests about human nature, society, values, or how the world works.

Proof (what happens)

"The Vicario brothers announce their plan publicly."

Payoff (what it means)

"This exposes honour as a performance directed at the community — the act only matters because it is witnessed."

Going further when the basics feel solid and you want to push your writing
How do I avoid turning this into a new template?

Simple test: if you can count your paragraph as "Sentence 1/2/3…", it's already a template in disguise. Keep your focus on function, not order. Look at the exemplars — evidence-first and claim-first paragraphs both work. What matters is that the claim, proof, payoff, and return are all doing real work.

I freeze when I don't have a checklist. What do I do?

Stay in Phase 1 until the functions feel automatic — there's no rush to remove the scaffold. The goal is for the four functions to become instinctive, not to write without support as early as possible. Use the Phase 3 reflection questions to check your own work once you're there.

How do I improve my writing between drafts?

One upgrade per draft. Identify the highest-leverage missing function — almost always the payoff line — and rewrite just that. Short, fast cycles work better than trying to fix everything at once. The exemplars and upgrade bank show exactly what each move looks like in practice.
